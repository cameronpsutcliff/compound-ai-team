# nod-protocol: Anti-Patterns

How this skill fails when applied lazily. Each anti-pattern names the
failure mode, the symptom, and the fix.

---

## Anti-pattern 1: Strawman opposite (Gate 2 failure)

**Symptom.** The opposite case sounds like something a defender would
never actually say. Rhetorical tells: "they just don't understand," "the
naive view is," "obviously this is wrong because." If the opposite
paragraph has these tells, it was written by the position's defender,
not by an opposite-side believer.

**Why it happens.** Writing the strongest opposite costs energy. The
brain offers a cheaper substitute: a weaker version that is easy to knock
down. The protocol catches this with the signing test.

**Fix.** Rewrite Gate 2 with this constraint: imagine the specific person
who would defend the opposite (a real or composite figure). Write in
their voice, with their evidence, with their tightest framing. Do not
write the opposite case while looking at the position. Close the
position, write the opposite from scratch, then re-open the position.

---

## Anti-pattern 2: Premature consensus (Gate 5 failure)

**Symptom.** "Both sides have a point, so the answer is probably
somewhere in the middle." This is averaging, not deciding. The middle
position has not survived a gate; it has only survived the discomfort
of choosing.

**Why it happens.** Decision feels harder than non-decision. Splitting
the difference feels like wisdom. It is usually evasion.

**Fix.** Force one of the three legitimate exits. Decide with stated
confidence. Defer with a specific test. Or refuse and route the
question upstream. The middle position is rarely a fourth exit; it is
usually a disguised refusal.

---

## Anti-pattern 3: Gate-skipping (ego-driven)

**Symptom.** The output shows gates 1, 2, and 5 but skips 3 and 4. Or
gates 3 and 4 are present but obviously written after gate 5 to justify
the conclusion.

**Why it happens.** The protocol's value is in the gates that hurt to
run. Gate 3 (shared assumption) and Gate 4 (falsifier) are the hardest
because they undermine the question itself. The temptation is to skip
them and produce a confident-looking output.

**Fix.** Treat the protocol as gated, not as a template. If gate 3
genuinely has no shared assumption (rare), say so explicitly and explain
why. If gate 4 has no testable falsifier (also rare), say so and reroute
to a different skill.

---

## Anti-pattern 4: Self-flattering opposite

**Symptom.** The opposite case is constructed to make the original
position look even better. Often the opposite is technically correct but
chooses a frame that the position can easily defeat.

**Why it happens.** Writing the opposite from inside the position's
framework. Same mental model, different conclusion. The opposite case
should usually use a different framework, not just a different answer
within the position's framework.

**Fix.** When writing Gate 2, change the frame, not just the conclusion.
If the position is framed in business terms, write the opposite in
operational or human terms. If the position is framed in long-run
metrics, write the opposite in short-run metrics. Frame-switching is
where real opposites live.

---

## Anti-pattern 5: Asymmetric falsifiers (Gate 4 failure)

**Symptom.** The position's falsifier is sharp and testable. The
opposite's falsifier is vague and unprovable. Or vice versa.

**Why it happens.** The author cares more about one side's case. The
side they care about gets a real test; the other side gets an escape
hatch.

**Fix.** Both falsifiers must be at the same level of specificity. If
one is "revenue grows by 15% in Q3," the other cannot be "things go
well." Rewrite both until they are symmetric.

---

## Anti-pattern 6: Performative refusal

**Symptom.** Every NOD output ends in "Exit C: Refuse." The refusal
becomes the safe answer.

**Why it happens.** Refusing feels intellectually rigorous. It is
sometimes correct (Gate 3 dominates the actual decision) but it is also
the safest exit, so it gets over-used.

**Fix.** Track your refusal rate. If more than ~30% of NOD runs end in
refusal, you are probably under-deciding. Force yourself to commit to
Exit A or Exit B unless the Gate 3 shared assumption is genuinely the
dominant question.

---

## Anti-pattern 7: NOD as performance art

**Symptom.** NOD is invoked on every minor question. The output is
verbose, the gates are real, but the protocol overhead exceeds the
decision's actual stakes.

**Why it happens.** A skill that feels rigorous gets over-applied.

**Fix.** Apply nod-protocol only when:
- The decision is hard to reverse, OR
- The decision affects multiple downstream choices, OR
- Multiple smart people disagree on it.

For reversible, low-blast-radius decisions, just decide and move.

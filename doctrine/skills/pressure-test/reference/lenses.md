# Pressure-Test Lenses
# Compound AI Operating Standards v3.0.7
# Source: cameronsutcliff.com/compound-ai | License: Apache 2.0

Ten executive operating lenses. Apply each to the work being reviewed. Each lens produces a verdict (PASS / REVISE / FAIL) and a specific refinement.

These are not business school principles. They are board-level operating discipline: what gets asked when the stakes are real and the time is short.

The ten lenses below are adapted from Michael Sutcliff's executive operating framework: board-level operating discipline distilled into mental models for reviewing work at the right altitude.

---

## 1. Multiples, not percentages

> Percentage improvements are management. Multiples are CEO work.

**Test:** Is this 20% better or 3x different?

If it's 20% better, delegate or automate it. If you can't articulate the multiple, you're not thinking at the right altitude.

**Verdict triggers:**
- PASS: "This eliminates the need for a 3-person reporting team"
- REVISE: "This saves 2 hours a week" → push to find the multiple
- FAIL: No multiple articulable

---

## 2. Horses → Cars → Trains

> Don't optimize the old paradigm.

**Test:** Which category is this?
- **Horse**  -  traditional approach, no structural change
- **Faster horse**  -  same process with AI bolted on
- **Car**  -  new architecture enabled by AI (automated, scalable, brand-compliant, searchable  -  impossible before)

Would this product or process exist if AI didn't exist? If yes, it's probably a faster horse.

**Verdict triggers:**
- PASS: Building the car (new structural primitive)
- REVISE: Faster horse with a clear car-version in sight
- FAIL: Pure horse  -  automating something that should be eliminated

---

## 3. The So-What Test (specificity)

> Every deliverable must answer three questions.

**Test:**
1. **Who** is the specific person using this? (Not "the business." A person.)
2. **What question** are they trying to answer?
3. **What decision** does it enable them to make?

A 40-page report nobody reads is waste. A 5-line summary that changes a partner's bid strategy is the whole point.

**Verdict triggers:**
- PASS: Named user, named question, named decision
- REVISE: Specific user but vague decision
- FAIL: "The business" is the user, or no downstream decision

---

## 4. Man + Machine = Multiples

> 30-40-50% faster alone is not the goal. Multiples are the goal.

**Test:** What is the agentic architecture? Specifically:
- Orchestrator decides task complexity and routes to the right model
- Token efficiency: spend only what the task requires
- Cost target: under $20/hour equivalent for the work being automated

**Verdict triggers:**
- PASS: Clear orchestrator, routed models, costed-out economics
- REVISE: Some automation, no orchestrator, no routing
- FAIL: Manual process being made marginally faster

---

## 5. Incentive Awareness

> Every person outside your immediate family is working in their best interests.

**Test:** Map every stakeholder's incentive. Does this plan account for what they actually want, not what they say they want?

This applies to: vendors, advisors, platform partners, customers, well-meaning mentors, internal stakeholders.

**Verdict triggers:**
- PASS: Incentive map explicit; plan designed around real incentives
- REVISE: Stakeholders identified but incentives not mapped
- FAIL: Plan assumes other parties act against their interests

---

## 6. Rational Decisions for Rational Reasons

> Emotional decisions feel urgent. Rational decisions come from data, incentive mapping, and second-order thinking.

**Test:**
- What is actually known vs. assumed?
- What does the data say vs. what does the narrative say?
- If urgency is removed, what does the decision look like?

**Red flag:** "We need to move fast on this" without a data-backed reason.

**Verdict triggers:**
- PASS: Decision survives removal of urgency framing
- REVISE: Urgency present but justified
- FAIL: Manufactured urgency or narrative-driven

---

## 7. Hope Is Not a Strategy

> A lot of people close their eyes and hope until the day they get the letter.

**Test:** For this initiative, name:
- The walk-away scenario
- The BATNA if the primary path fails
- The early signal that tells you it's failing  -  BEFORE it's obvious

**Verdict triggers:**
- PASS: All three explicit
- REVISE: Walk-away exists but BATNA undefined
- FAIL: No defined failure signal  -  operating on hope

---

## 8. The Iceberg Test

> Is this melting?

**Test:** Is the platform / technology / market this depends on melting?
- **Melting icebergs:** traditional directories, manual analytics, generalist SaaS, legacy reporting
- **Growing landmasses:** agentic AI platforms, niche vertical intelligence, man + machine architectures

Don't end your life on a melting iceberg. Know your exit condition before you need it.

**Verdict triggers:**
- PASS: Built on growing landmass with named exit condition
- REVISE: Built on stable ice with no exit plan
- FAIL: Built on melting iceberg with no awareness

---

## 9. Chapters of Life

> What does the next chapter require?

**Test:** Is this decision optimized for the current chapter (which is ending) or the next chapter (which is starting)?

Don't confuse momentum with the right answer. The right answer for last year may be the wrong answer for next year.

**Verdict triggers:**
- PASS: Plan is for the next chapter explicitly
- REVISE: Plan optimizes current chapter but is consistent with next
- FAIL: Plan locks in patterns the next chapter will reject

---

## 10. Domain Ownership

> Go deep, not wide.

**Test:** For every agent, team, or subsystem in this plan:
- One clear mandate (one domain)
- Structured inputs / outputs
- Self-validating (checks its own quality)
- Composable (output slots into the next tier's input)

Generalists drift. Domain owners compound.

**Verdict triggers:**
- PASS: Every component has one mandate and structured I/O
- REVISE: Mandates clear but I/O contracts loose
- FAIL: Generalist roles with overlapping responsibilities

---

## How to use this file

The pressure-test skill walks all ten lenses. Some lenses don't apply (e.g. lens 4 for a marketing campaign critique). Mark those N/A and move on. The point is to apply every relevant lens and produce a verdict, not to force-fit every lens to every situation.

When a lens fires (REVISE or FAIL), the refinement should be SPECIFIC. Not "consider the multiple"  -  "this plan saves 2 hours per week per analyst across 50 analysts; reframe the multiple as 100 hours/week of analyst capacity reclaimed and re-evaluate whether 3x is achievable."

Specificity is the difference between a useful critique and a generic one.

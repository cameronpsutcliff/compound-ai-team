# agent-panel-review: When to Convene

A panel costs time, attention, and orchestration discipline. The
threshold for convening should be deliberately high. Most work does
not need a panel.

---

## Convene a panel when at least one is true

1. **The deliverable is high-stakes and durable.** A field guide, a
   foreword, a public release, a strategic plan, a major decision
   that will be hard to walk back. Things that benefit from
   multi-pass review before they reach an audience.

2. **The work spans multiple genuinely-different functions.** Voice
   + substance + architecture. Or builder + critic + integrator. The
   distinct functions cannot be served well by one agent without
   anchoring on one of them.

3. **The decision is irreversible at meaningful cost.** Schema
   migrations, public commitments, contract terms, public
   attributions. A panel surfaces blind spots before they ship.

4. **You suspect groupthink or convergence theater.** When the
   single-agent output reads too smooth, too confident, too
   uncontested. A panel breaks the smoothness by forcing real
   disagreement to surface.

5. **The work explicitly requires multiple perspectives.** Stakeholder
   maps, cross-platform translation, policy drafts that have to land
   for multiple audiences.

---

## Do NOT convene a panel when

1. **The work is routine code or a single answer.** "How do I do X in
   Python" does not need a panel. Just answer.

2. **One good prompt would have done it.** If the operator can write a
   prompt that produces a complete first-pass output, the panel is
   overhead.

3. **The decision is reversible at low cost.** Trying a refactor for
   an hour and reverting is cheaper than running a panel on whether to
   refactor.

4. **Time pressure is real and the convergence cost would exceed the
   accuracy gain.** A panel takes time. A wrong answer in 30 minutes
   is sometimes better than a right answer in 4 hours.

5. **You already know what you want.** A panel that produces three
   drafts of a foregone conclusion is performative. If your mind is
   made up, just write it.

---

## The convergence-theater test

Run this test before convening a panel:

> "If I imagine the panel converging on loop 1 with all three agents
> agreeing with my current instinct, would I find that valuable?"

If yes: you do not need a panel. You need confirmation, which a panel
is bad at producing. Or you need to learn to trust your instinct.

If no (you specifically want the panel to disagree with your instinct
and surface what you are missing): convene the panel.

---

## The opportunity-cost test

Run this test before convening a panel:

> "What else could I produce in the time this panel will take?"

A panel for a foreword typically takes 2-4 hours of operator time
across loops. If the operator's next-best use of those hours produces
more total value than the foreword improvement will, the panel is the
wrong call.

This is not a reason to avoid panels. It is a reason to convene them
only when the deliverable genuinely warrants the time.

---

## The "did I need a panel" retrospective

After a panel ships, run a short retrospective:

1. **Did the panel surface blind spots I would not have found alone?**
   If yes, the panel earned its cost. If no, it was overhead.
2. **Did the convergence happen too fast?** Convergence on loop 1
   usually means the question was answerable without a panel.
3. **Did the convergence happen too slowly?** Four-plus loops usually
   means the question was framed too broadly.
4. **Would I run a panel on this kind of deliverable again?** This is
   the calibration question. Update the threshold.

Over time, the operator's threshold for convening should sharpen.
Early in panel-use, you will convene too often. After a dozen panels,
you will know.
